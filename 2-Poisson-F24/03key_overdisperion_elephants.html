  1. Conduct a goodness of fit test for the Poisson regression model.
1 - pchisq(pois.elephant$deviance, pois.elephant$df.residual)
## [1] 0.09426231

\(H0:\) Model fits well (no overdispersion), \(HA\): Lack of fit (Possibly due to overdispersion, or for other reasons!)

p-value is 0.09, so we fail to reject H0. There is not evidnce of overdispersion.

  1. Would you choose a quasi-Poisson or Poisson model for these elephant data? Are there any downsides to choosing the quasi-Poisson model?

As the overdispersion is only estimated to be 1.15, this is not a lot of overdispersion! The overdispersed model is slightly more conservative, and so may be preferred even if the amount of overdispersion is small. (In this case, we see the effect of age is obviously statistically significant, whether or not overdispersion is accounted for).

The downside of choosing the quasi-Poisson model is that we do not get an AIC to use for non-nested comparisons.

THere is significant evidence of lack of fit (p-value = 0.0008).

Lack of fit can occur for many reasons!

  • outliers
  • important variables are missing, or relationships are actually nonlinear
  • observations are not truly independent

If you can adapt your model to account for any of these, DO THAT FIRST. Then, if all else fails, account for overdispersion with a quasi-Poisson or negative binomial model.

Soccer goals: Negative binomial model

Another approach to accounting for overdisperson is the Negative Binomial model. The interpretation of regression coefficients is the same as in the Poisson and Quasi-Poisson models. The distributional assumption is slightly different (negative binomial rather than Poisson), and the relationship between mean and variance is thus different (more of a quadratic relationship).
